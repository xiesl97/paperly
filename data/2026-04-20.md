Abstract: Secondary eclipse observations are a powerful way to investigate whether or not a rocky exoplanet hosts an atmosphere, as an atmospheric presence would transport heat to the nightside and render the dayside colder than anticipated. The interpretation of the secondary eclipse observations relies, however, on models based on imperfect knowledge of the host star properties and the system parameters. Any uncertainties in such astrophysical variables will propagate into both atmospheric and bare-rock models, potentially leading to poorly constrained results and erroneous conclusions.
